TL;DR

The PRFAQ was designed in 2004 to replace expensive prototypes with cheap documents. That constraint no longer exists. This playbook replaces the PRFAQ with a four-phase process that gets from idea to real customer feedback and a build decision in 5 days ‚Äî not 5 weeks.

Why the PRFAQ Is Holding Us Back

Amazon invented Working Backwards because in 2004, building a prototype was expensive and slow. Writing a press release was cheap. That tradeoff no longer exists ‚Äî but the process stuck.

‚ùå The Problem

Teams spend weeks perfecting prose about a product no one has seen. By the time a customer reacts, too much conviction (and sometimes code) has been invested to kill it honestly. The "customer quote" in the PR is fiction. The FAQs answer imagined objections, not real ones. Review meetings are document debates, not decision meetings.

‚úÖ The Opportunity

We can now show customers a working prototype the same day an idea is formed, pull real signal from Salesforce and Notion before writing a single word, and make a build decision based on actual customer reactions ‚Äî not internal consensus on document quality.

The Four Phases

1
Signal Brief ‚Äî Pull the Data
Day 1 ¬∑ ~2 hours

Old process equivalent: Locking the PM in a room to write the first press release draft.

Before anyone writes a word, pull existing signal from systems we already have. The goal is to replace the PM's hypothesis with evidence.

  • Salesforce: Closed-lost deals citing the relevant feature gap, sorted by MRR impact ‚Äî how much revenue is the missing capability costing us right now?
  • Notion Onboarding: Active clients blocked from graduating due to the same gap ‚Äî what's the retention risk?
  • Outreach / Call Notes: Verbatim customer language about the pain ‚Äî these become the "customer quotes" (real ones)
  • Support tickets / CS notes: Frequency of the ask across the active base

Output: A one-page Signal Brief ‚Äî problem statement, supporting data, MRR at risk, verbatim customer language. Takes 2 hours. Replaces a week of writing.

2
Prototype ‚Äî Build Something Real
Days 1‚Äì2 ¬∑ Same day

Old process equivalent: Writing the Public FAQ and Internal FAQ over multiple drafts.

Instead of describing what the product will be, show it. The goal is something a customer can react to ‚Äî not something that's production-ready.

  • Clickable prototype (Figma / v0.dev): Key user flows only. 4‚Äì8 screens. Built same day.
  • Narrated demo video: 2‚Äì3 minute screen recording walking through the prototype as if it's shipped. This replaces the press release entirely ‚Äî it communicates better, to every stakeholder, in less time.
  • Functional proof-of-concept for data/API features: Even a rough working version beats a description of one.

Bar to clear: Can someone watch this and understand what we're building and why it matters? If yes, move on. If not, iterate for another half day ‚Äî not another week.

3
Customer Validation ‚Äî Real Reactions
Days 2‚Äì4 ¬∑ 3 calls

Old process equivalent: Stakeholder review meeting where everyone reads the document silently for 20 minutes.

Show the prototype to 3 customers within the same week. Prioritize:

  • One current client who's been asking for this capability
  • One closed-lost deal where this gap was cited as the reason
  • One ICP prospect currently evaluating Rev.io

The calls are 30 minutes. Show, don't tell. Ask: "What would you expect to happen next?" and "Would this change your decision?" Record everything.

Output: 3 real customer reactions that either validate, modify, or kill the idea ‚Äî plus verbatims to include in the Product Brief. The FAQs now answer real questions.

4
Product Brief + Build Gate
Day 5 ¬∑ 1 meeting

Old process equivalent: Final PRFAQ review with sign-off from 10+ stakeholders over multiple iterations.

After customer validation, then you write ‚Äî but it's a 1-page Product Brief, not a 3-page narrative. Structured, not prose. Every brief must answer all seven fields.

  • Problem: Explicit statement of what specific pain or friction we are solving ‚Äî in customer language, not internal language. Supported by verbatims from Phase 1 + Phase 3 calls.
  • Client Value: What will the customer be able to do, save, or earn that they cannot today? Stated in concrete terms: time saved, revenue unlocked, churn prevented, manual steps eliminated. Not "improved experience" ‚Äî a real measurable outcome.
  • AI / Agentic Angle: Every feature considered must explicitly answer: Where does AI or automation make this smarter, faster, or self-driving? This is not optional. If there is no AI/agentic tie-in, the brief must explain why and whether one should be explored before building the baseline. Examples: auto-classification, predictive alerts, agent-assisted workflows, AI-drafted outputs, anomaly detection, self-healing processes.
  • Solution: Prototype link + 2-sentence description of what we're building
  • Customer reaction: What the 3 calls told us (real quotes from Phase 3)
  • Investment ask: Engineering estimate + MRR at risk / retention impact side by side
  • MVP scope: Minimum viable version for next Apollo cycle ‚Äî one sentence max

One decision meeting. One outcome.

The Build Gate ‚Äî Three Questions

Every idea goes through a single decision meeting with one clear output: build it, reshape it, or kill it.

üìã Build Gate Questions

1
Did customers react the way we expected? What surprised us? Did they validate the problem or redirect us to a different one?
2
Is the MRR/retention impact worth the engineering cost? Quantified: dollars at risk vs. sprint capacity. Not a gut call.
3
What's the minimum shippable version in one Apollo cycle? If we can't define it clearly in a sentence, scope it down until we can.

If all three answers are confident: the feature goes into Apollo planning with a defined MVP scope and owner. If any answer is unclear: either run one more customer call or kill the idea. No zombie features.
